Gemma 4 26B-A4B MoE

A 25.2 B-total / 3.8 B-active Sparse Mixture-of-Experts decoder built from Gemini 3 research, released April 2, 2026 under Apache 2.0. The 26B-A4B sits between the edge E2B/E4B models and the dense 31B flagship: same Gemma 4 family backbone — local sliding-window attention interleaved with full global attention, p-RoPE and unified KV on the global layers — but the dense MLP at every layer is replaced by a 128-expert top-8 MoE block. That gives 26 B of capacity at roughly 4 B of per-token compute, which is the central architectural trade. The HF mirror lists the per-checkpoint expert tensors as a fused [128, dim, dim] 3-D parameter, not a list of nn.Linear — a layout that has tripped up several community quantisers.

02 · End-to-end map

From multimodal input to a text logit vector — with routing in the middle.

A prompt arrives as an interleaved sequence of text tokens and image regions. Images are lifted into the language-model token stream by a ~550 M vision encoder; the joint sequence then passes through 30 decoder layers and exits as logits over the 262 K Gemma vocabulary. Inside each block, the feed-forward step is no longer a single dense MLP — a router scores 128 experts per token and dispatches the hidden state to the top 8. Audio in is not supported on 26B-A4B; only the E2B and E4B models embed an audio encoder.

03 · The 30-layer stack

Half the depth of the 31B Dense, same local-global rhythm.

Gemma 4 26B-A4B has 30 transformer layers — exactly half the depth of the 31B Dense. The Gemma 4 model card states that every variant in the family interleaves local sliding-window attention with full global attention and that the final layer is always global. Community model cards derived from the released config (e.g. the jenerallee78 mirror used by several quantisation projects) report the 26B-A4B layout as 25 sliding + 5 full, i.e. a 5 : 1 local-to-global cadence. The Google model card text we fetched names the rule but does not publish the per-layer pattern, so the ribbon below visualises the 5 L : 1 G cadence as a community-reported placeholder.

04 · Decoder block internals

Pre-norm transformer block — but the MLP is a 128-expert MoE.

Externally the 26B-A4B block looks like a standard Gemma block: pre-norm arrangement, RMSNorm, grouped-query attention with QK-Norm, residual paths around attention and feed-forward. The difference sits in the feed-forward sub-block: instead of one SwiGLU MLP, a router scores the hidden state against 128 experts, picks the top-8, runs the SwiGLU on each, and combines the results weighted by the router probabilities. The model card does not re-state head counts, hidden size, or expert hidden width for the 26B-A4B, so the diagram below marks numeric shapes as not reported where the fetched model-card text is silent. The HF community discussion does confirm the experts are stored as a single nn.Parameter of shape [128, dim, dim] per layer — not as a nn.ModuleList of 128 linears.

1 · Pre-attention RMSNorm
RMSNorm

Pre-norm transformer arrangement: normalize the residual stream before entering the attention sub-block. Learned scale γ, no bias.

2 · Grouped-query attention
GQA · inventory lead

Multiple query heads share each KV head. Head counts for the 26B-A4B are not stated in the fetched model card; GQA itself is the Raschka-table attribute carried forward from Gemma 3.

3 · QK-Norm
per-head RMSNorm

RMSNorm applied to the per-head queries and keys before the dot product. Stabilises logits at long context.

4 · Local or global attention
window 1 024 · or · full

Layer type is fixed at config time. Local attends only to the most recent 1 024 tokens; global attends over the full 256 K window and additionally applies p-RoPE and unified KV.

5 · Residual add
x + Attn(x)

Standard residual connection around the attention sub-block.

6 · Router · softmax(W_r · h)
128 logits → top-8

A small linear layer scores the hidden state against 128 experts. Top-8 are selected per token; the router probabilities of the chosen experts are renormalised and become combination weights.

7 · SwiGLU experts × top-8
fused [128, d, d] tensor

Each expert is a SwiGLU MLP — W_down(SiLU(W_gate·h) ⊙ (W_up·h)) — stored together as a single 3-D parameter per layer. Only the eight selected experts run; their outputs are summed weighted by router probabilities.

8 · Residual add
x + MoE(x)

Residual closes the block. Output goes to the next layer or, at layer 30, to the final RMSNorm + LM head.

05 · 128-expert top-8 routing

Every token visits eight of one hundred and twenty-eight experts.

The MoE block is the architectural difference between 26B-A4B and the 31B Dense flagship — and the reason it can match a much larger model's quality at ~4 B of per-token compute. Each block holds 128 SwiGLU experts; the router picks the top-8 for each token independently, so different tokens take different paths through the same layer.

The specific expert indices shown above are illustrative — Google does not publish per-token routing traces; the model card describes only the routing geometry, not which expert specialises in what. Routes change per layer and per token: a token that visits {7, 23, …} at layer 4 will likely visit a different set at layer 5.

Inside one MoE block

Router scoring → top-k gather → weighted combine

Routing. A token's hidden state h_t is scored against 128 expert vectors by a small linear layer W_r ∈ ℝ^(d × 128). The 128 softmax probabilities are sorted and the top 8 indices are kept. Their probabilities are renormalised to sum to 1 and become combination weights.

Compute. Only those 8 experts actually run on this token; the other 120 contribute nothing to the forward (or backward) pass for h_t. Different tokens in the same minibatch take different routes, so the aggregate compute hits all experts roughly proportional to their popularity.

Fused storage. Each layer stores its 128 experts as a single 3-D nn.Parameter rather than a list of linears. The community quantisation note flags that this "breaks all existing quantization tools" — modelopt, llm-compressor, and TensorRT-LLM all expect a ModuleList of Linears and silently skip the fused expert parameters, which are ≈ 91 % of the model.

MoE block · sketch
s = softmax( W_r · h ) ∈ ℝ^128
I = top-k indices of s, k = 8
g = s[I] / Σ s[I] // renormalised weights
y = Σk g_k · SwiGLUE_k(h)

07 · Local vs global attention

Two attention regimes alternating along the stack.

The Gemma 4 model card describes the family attention as "a hybrid attention mechanism that interleaves local sliding window attention with full global attention, ensuring the final layer is always global. This hybrid design delivers the processing speed and low memory footprint of a lightweight model without sacrificing the deep awareness required for complex, long-context tasks." The two regimes share QKV projection geometry but differ in mask, positional encoding, and KV caching. The 26B-A4B inherits this design unchanged from the rest of Gemma 4.

25 of 30 layers

Local sliding-window attention

Each query attends only over the most recent 1 024 tokens of context (family default per the model card). Cheap, constant-memory per token, regardless of how long the full prompt is.

08 · p-RoPE & unified KV

Two memory tricks that ride on the five global layers.

The Gemma 4 model card calls out two long-context optimisations applied only to the global layers: Proportional RoPE (p-RoPE) for positional encoding, and unified Keys and Values in the KV cache. Local layers do not carry these. The model card introduces the names without a numerical recipe, so this section presents the behaviour the names imply and labels the formal definitions as not reported.

Long-context memory · global layers only

Proportional RoPE + shared K / V

p-RoPE. The Gemma 4 model card names Proportional RoPE as the positional encoding used on global layers. The precise frequency-scaling recipe is not stated in the fetched text, but the model-card phrasing pairs p-RoPE explicitly with long-context behaviour, suggesting per-dimension base frequencies scaled with context length.

Unified Keys and Values. Global layers tie the K and V projections so they share storage in the KV cache, roughly halving the per-token cache footprint at those layers. With only 5 global layers out of 30, the cache footprint at 256 K context is dominated by these layers — so the saving compounds where it matters.

Why only on global layers? Global attention is the bottleneck at long context — its KV cache grows with the full sequence. Local layers cap at the 1 024-token window, so the cache there is already bounded; the optimisations target the layers where memory actually hurts.

Global-layer attention · sketch
K = V = W_KV · h // unified projection
Q = W_Q · h
Q', K' = p-RoPE(Q, K, pos) // proportional rotation
A = softmax( Q' · K'ᵀ / √d_h ) · V // causal · full context
Exact head dim, frequency base, and projection shapes — not reported in the fetched Gemma 4 model-card text.
09 · Vision tower

Variable-resolution image input as a token budget.

The Gemma 4 26B-A4B shares the family ~550 M-parameter vision encoder with the 31B Dense, which turns images and video frames into visual tokens prepended to the language stream. The number of tokens per image is configurable — a budget of 70, 140, 280, 560, or 1 120 — letting callers trade detail for throughput. Multimodal prompts should place image content before text. Audio is not supported on the 26B-A4B (only the E2B / E4B variants embed an audio encoder).

10 · Thinking mode

A built-in reasoning channel, toggleable per prompt.

Gemma 4 26B-A4B is shipped with the same configurable thinking mode as the rest of the family: include a <|think|> token at the start of the system prompt and the model writes its internal reasoning into a separate channel before producing the final answer. Thought content must not be carried into subsequent turns.

13 · Caveats & unknowns

What the fetched model card does not say.

The Gemma 4 model card is dense at the family / capability level but sparse at the per-shape level. Several quantities a deployer or researcher would want for Gemma 4 26B-A4B specifically — especially the MoE hyperparameters — are not in the fetched text and have been left as not reported throughout this page.

Expert hidden width

The fused expert tensor has shape [128, d_in, d_h] per layer per the HF community thread, but neither d_in (model dim) nor d_h (expert intermediate width) is published in the fetched model-card text. Confirm from config.json on the HF repo before quoting.

Router design

Top-8 of 128 is confirmed by the launch blog and community model cards, but the router scoring function (plain softmax vs softmax-and-renorm vs gated linear), the load-balancing loss coefficient, and whether a shared / always-on expert exists are not stated in the fetched text.

Hidden size and head geometry

The model card does not state d_model, n_heads, n_kv_heads, or head_dim for the 26B-A4B. The HF config file is the canonical source.

Local / global interleave ratio

The 25-sliding + 5-full count comes from a community-derived model card. The Google model card text confirms only the rule that the final layer is global. The exact positions of the 5 global layers within the 30-layer stack are not stated.

Positional encoding on local layers

p-RoPE is specified for global layers. The positional scheme used on local layers is not separately described; whether it is also p-RoPE, vanilla RoPE, or implicit-via-window is unclear from the fetched text.

Expert parallelism & serving

The vLLM recipe runs the 26B-A4B on a single accelerator with --max-model-len 32768 in the published example. Recommended expert-parallel layouts, capacity factor at serving time, and routing-imbalance handling are not stated.

Training token budget & MoE recipe

The model card describes the data mix and cutoff (Jan 2025) but not the token count, optimiser, MoE balancing-loss weight, distillation recipe, or compute. The launch blog credits Gemini 3 research without giving numbers.

Quantisation

BF16 is the base release type. The HF community thread documents that the fused 3-D expert tensor breaks modelopt, llm-compressor, and TensorRT-LLM, which all expect a ModuleList of Linears and silently skip the fused parameters. Community NVFP4 W4A4 and W4A16 quantisations exist for this checkpoint but rely on a custom unfuse step.
