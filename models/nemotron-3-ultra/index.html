Nemotron 3 Ultra

The largest open model in NVIDIA's Nemotron 3 family — a 550-billion-parameter hybrid Mamba‑Transformer mixture-of-experts (MoE) reasoning engine with 55 B parameters active per token, a 1 M-token context window, and built-in speculative decoding via multi-token prediction heads. Pre-trained natively in 4-bit NVFP4 on NVIDIA Blackwell. Shipped first as a pre-training base checkpoint for downstream customization.
Hybrid Mamba-Transformer MoE LatentMoE Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) NVFP4 pre-training 1 M-token context Base only — no post-training yet Weights pending 1H 2026

Nemotron 3 Ultra is positioned as NVIDIA's large-scale reasoning engine within an agentic stack: Nano (30 B/A3B) for high-throughput sub-agents, Super (120 B/A12B) for collaborative multi-agent workloads, and Ultra (550 B/A55B) for complex planning and deep analysis. All three share the same backbone — Mamba-2 selective state-space layers interleaved with grouped-query attention layers and LatentMoE feed-forward layers — and differ primarily in scale.

Section 03

Hybrid block structure

NVIDIA describes Nemotron 3 as a Mixture-of-Experts hybrid Mamba‑Transformer architecture. The exact interleaving pattern (how many Mamba‑2, attention, and MoE layers, and in what order) is published for Nemotron 3 Nano, but it has not been disclosed for Ultra in the source inventory. Mamba-2 layers

Selective state-space layers that mix information along the sequence axis in linear time. NVIDIA explicitly credits Mamba-2 with making the 1 M-token context window "practical for long-document and agentic workloads."

Update rule:

Attention layers

Standard transformer attention is interleaved sparsely between the Mamba‑2 layers to preserve precise token-to-token interaction that pure SSMs lack. The Nemotron 3 white paper labels the backbone "MoE hybrid Mamba‑Transformer", confirming attention is part of every layer pattern.

LatentMoE feed-forward

Sparse feed-forward layer that compresses each token into a low-rank latent space before routing, then dispatches the compressed representation to a small subset of many specialist experts. This produces the 10 % active-parameter ratio (55 B / 550 B).

expert count, top-k for Ultra unknown

For context only: Nemotron 3 Nano (30 B / A3B) is published as 23 Mamba‑2 layers + 23 LatentMoE layers + 6 grouped-query attention layers, with 128 experts and top-6 routing. The Ultra layer-pattern equivalents are not in the source inventory and are not assumed here.
Section 04

LatentMoE

LatentMoE is the architectural lever that lets Ultra hold 550 B parameters at 55 B active per token. Conventional MoE routes the full hidden state through a chosen expert; LatentMoE first projects the hidden state to a low-rank latent, then routes and expert-applies in the compressed space. The smaller per-expert compute lets NVIDIA pack roughly 4× as many expert specialists for the same inference budget.

Why latent compression matters

Per-token FLOPs through an expert are dominated by the matmul against expert weights. Routing in the compressed space r ≪ d reduces those FLOPs proportional to r/d, which is what frees the budget for additional specialists.

Effective parameter ratio

For Ultra, the published 55 B / 550 B figure implies roughly 10 % of weights active per token. That number includes Mamba-2 and attention layers in addition to the MoE experts; the per-layer sparsity is not separately reported.

Section 05

Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)

NVIDIA describes Multi-Token Prediction as predicting multiple future tokens in a single forward pass. The auxiliary heads serve two roles: they improve representations (encouraging the trunk to capture longer-range dependencies during pre-training) and they act as an internal draft model for speculative decoding at inference time, eliminating the need for an external draft model.

From the cookbook
"Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) — predicts multiple future tokens in a single forward pass, improving chain-of-thought coherence and enabling built-in speculative decoding at inference time."
Section 06

Mamba-2 and the 1 M-token context path

Attention is quadratic in sequence length; that is what makes a 1 M-token context window expensive in a pure transformer. Nemotron 3 Ultra carries most of the sequence-mixing work in Mamba‑2 selective state-space layers, which run in linear time and use a fixed-size recurrent state — no KV cache to grow with context. NVIDIA states this directly: "Mamba‑2 layers provide linear-time complexity over sequence length, making 1 M-token context practical for long-document and agentic workloads."

Why this matters for Ultra

Sequence compute O(L) linear, not quadratic
Inference state O(1) recurrent — no KV cache
Max context 1,048,576 tokens, verified
Attention role sparse preserves precise global mixing where Mamba‑2 alone is weak

The attention layers do still have a KV cache, but at a much smaller per-layer rate than a pure-transformer model would — exact grouping (MHA vs. GQA vs. MQA) and head counts are not reported for Ultra.

Section 07

Training and precision

Nemotron 3 Ultra is shipped as a pre-training base checkpoint. NVIDIA explicitly notes it has not undergone instruction tuning or post-training alignment — that is positioned as downstream work for adopters who will fine-tune, run RLHF, or build custom instruction pipelines on top of the base.

Pre-training precision

Pre-trained natively in NVFP4, NVIDIA's 4-bit floating-point format optimised for Blackwell. The Nemotron 3 white paper claims this delivers "stable and accurate pre-training in low precision."

Important caveat
Nemotron 3 Nano and Super are post-trained with a multi-environment reinforcement learning pipeline described in the Nemotron 3 white paper, and Super's model card details asynchronous RL with in-flight weight updates and MTP-accelerated rollout generation. None of that is confirmed for the Ultra base release. Until NVIDIA publishes Ultra's technical report, treat Ultra's post-training pipeline as undisclosed.
